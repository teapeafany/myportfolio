00: The Story

For most of us, it's invisible.

Background noise while commuting, studying, cooking. But that invisibility is exactly what makes the interruptions so jarring. A playlist ends. The mood shifts. You're suddenly scrolling, tapping, searching for something that matches a feeling you can't quite name.

This case study documents my journey of taking something I love, music listening, and picking it apart to uncover the quiet frustrations hidden in an otherwise familiar experience.

01: Project Overview

What is Echosphere?

Echosphere is a conceptual music-discovery experience we developed, targeted towards all music listeners ages 18-24, focused on breaking algorithmic filter bubbles and introducing a genuine exploratory experience.

üéß So.. what's the deal with music?

Current music platforms like Spotify and Apple Music overwhelmingly rely on recommendation algorithms that recycle familiar content, limiting genuine discovery and weakening emotional engagement with music.

Users reported feeling disconnected from artists, overwhelmed by surface-level recommendations, and constrained by systems that rarely reflect their mood, headspace, or social environment.

üí° My Role

I led a team of 2 UX researchers, guiding research synthesis, collaborative ideation sessions, iterative prototyping (lo-fi through hi-fi), and weekly design critiques to refine our solution.

02: The Research

üîç Observing the Problem Space

To understand how people discover and engage with music, we conducted interviews and surveys in September 2024.

We received 40 survey responses and conducted 6 in-depth interviews with Georgia Tech students ages 18-24 who regularly use streaming platforms.

The insights from these conversations revealed patterns in music discovery habits, emotional responses to recommendations, and social sharing behaviors that shaped our design direction.

‚ùì Interview Insights

üí°

What makes a song listenable?

+

The concept of immersion emerged as a key factor. Participants described moments where music "pulls you out of your thoughts" and creates a deep, uninterrupted engagement. One participant noted: "I felt it way more than when I was just listening."

Concert experiences were highlighted as particularly immersive, offering lasting and memorable connections to songs and artists. The difference between listening to an album at home versus experiencing it live was profound‚Äîlive performances created emotional connections that persisted long after the event.

Beyond immersion, participants also valued attention to detail: the quality of beats, the complexity of instrumentals, and the depth of lyrics all contributed to a song's "listenability."

üí°

What determines immediate music taste?

+

Participants revealed that immediate music preferences are heavily influenced by contextual factors. Weather, mood, and environment all play significant roles in what someone wants to listen to at any given moment.

Variability was another key theme‚Äîusers crave diversity in their listening experience. One participant explained how scrolling on TikTok exposes them to new sounds, creating what they called a "sound stim" that influences their immediate preferences.

The phenomenon of songs getting "stuck in your head" was also mentioned as a determinant of immediate taste. When a melody or lyric resonates, it creates an urge to listen to that specific song, often overriding algorithmic recommendations.

üîç Applying the Research

To systematically organize insights from our interviews, we applied thematic analysis using coding and affinity mapping. We transcribed interviews in Taguette, tagged recurring patterns, and grouped similar codes in FigJam to identify broader themes.

This process helped us move from individual observations to actionable design insights.

4

Key Themes

From our analysis, three key themes emerged that shaped our design direction:

üéµ Boredom with Familiar Songs

What We Heard: Users reported getting tired of the same recommendations and craved novelty and freshness in their listening experience.

Design Implication: Design for serendipity and anti-repetition mechanics

üîç Algorithm Reliance vs. Active Exploration

What We Heard: While many users depend on algorithmic suggestions, some actively seek new artists and genres as an intentional, enjoyable activity.

Design Implication: Balance passive discovery with exploratory tools that give users agency

üåç Context Matters

What We Heard: Users differentiate between casual background listening and focused engagement. Platforms ignore mood, environment, and social setting.

Design Implication: Build context-aware features that adapt to how users actually listen

03: The Problems

‚ö†Ô∏è Identifying Key Pain Points

Music platforms feel effortless until they don't. Small disruptions‚Äîskipping repeatedly, losing your place, feeling overwhelmed by choice‚Äîreveal friction hiding behind something meant to be relaxing.

üîÑ Pain Point #1

Algorithmic repetition limits discovery. Recommendation systems recycle familiar content, trapping users in filter bubbles that reinforce what they already know instead of introducing something new.

üíî Pain Point #2

Emotional disconnect from artists. Discovering music feels transactional. Without storytelling or context, users struggle to form meaningful connections with the artists behind the songs.

üå´Ô∏è Pain Point #3

Context blindness. Platforms ignore mood, environment, and social setting. Recommendations feel generic because the system knows listening history but not the listener's current headspace.

04: User Needs

üí° User Need: A Need for Music Novelty

We determined that users consistently expressed a need for novelty in their listening experiences. Many participants reported getting bored of familiar songs, wanting songs to match their changing moods, and sought out new music as a way to refresh their playlists and maintain engagement. This desire manifested in two distinct approaches:

  • Algorithm-Reliant Listeners: the users who primarily depend on algorithmic recommendations or passive discovery through social media and friends.
  • Active Explorers: the users who dedicate time to actively searching for new artists and songs, treating the process as an enjoyable, intentional activity.

üí° User Needs: A Need for Immersive Listening

Participants value moments of deep, uninterrupted engagement with music. Immersion was often described as a state where the listener is "pulled out of their thoughts" and fully absorbed in the sound.

Concerts and live performances were highlighted as strong triggers of immersion, offering lasting and memorable connections to songs and artists. Participants suggested that such immersive experiences counterbalance the fast-paced, disposable nature of trending sounds, creating opportunities for more meaningful and enduring relationships with music.

üí° User Needs: Social Media's Influence

We found that users' listening choices are strongly shaped by external influences, both algorithmic (social media posts/spotify-generated algorithm) and contextual.

These algorithms were described as convenient entry points for discovery, though they can create limitations by reinforcing familiar patterns. Participants also noted the frequency bias, where repeated exposure to a song (through social media's trending reels) increased its likability over time.

üí°

The Insight

Current platforms optimize for prediction, not exploration. Users don't just want the algorithm to guess what they'll like.

They want agency in discovering music that matches how they actually feel.

How might we create a music discovery experience that breaks algorithmic filter bubbles and rebuilds meaningful connections between artists and listeners?

05: Our Design Features

üåê Feature: Community Discovery Spaces

Instead of AI-generated suggestions, users can explore the music spheres of people around them, friends, peers, or even strangers nearby. This supports authentic discovery through human taste and shared moments, fulfilling users' need for subtle, meaningful social connection without overwhelming feeds or performative social pressure.

Design Goal: Enable Human-Centered, Non-Algorithmic Social Discovery

‚ú® Feature: User Spheres

User Spheres create immersive, story-driven spaces crafted by artists and friends. By offering behind-the-scenes content, personal updates, and curated music bubbles that they can share with their fans and friends, this feature directly responds to users' desire for deeper context and stronger emotional ties with the artists they follow.

Design Goal: Deepen Artist-Listener Relationships

üåô Feature: Mood & Environment Spheres

Users reported that algorithmic playlists feel shallow and disconnected from their actual mood or environment. Mood & Environment Spheres use contextual cues (weather, time, location, and listening habits) to generate more personal, emotionally resonant discovery paths. These spheres adapt through interactive 'Vibe checks,' giving users control over how their exploration evolves.

Design Goal: Create Emotionally Intelligent Music Discovery
