00: The Story

For most of us, it's invisible.

Background noise while commuting, studying, cooking. But that invisibility is exactly what makes the interruptions so jarring. A playlist ends. The mood shifts. You're suddenly scrolling, tapping, searching for something that matches a feeling you can't quite name.

This case study documents my journey of taking something I love, music listening, and picking it apart to uncover the quiet frustrations hidden in an otherwise familiar experience.

01: Project Overview

Overview

Echosphere is a conceptual music-discovery experience we developed, targeted towards all music listeners ages 18-24, focused on breaking algorithmic filter bubbles and introducing a genuine exploratory experience.

The Context

Current music platforms like Spotify and Apple Music overwhelmingly rely on recommendation algorithms that recycle familiar content, limiting genuine discovery and weakening emotional engagement with music. Users reported feeling disconnected from artists, overwhelmed by surface-level recommendations, and constrained by systems that rarely reflect their mood, headspace, or social environment.

02: The Problems

Music platforms feel effortless until they don't. Small disruptions—skipping repeatedly, losing your place, feeling overwhelmed by choice—reveal friction hiding behind something meant to be relaxing.

I wanted to understand: why do these moments feel so disruptive, even when the technology works as intended?

Pain Point #1

Algorithmic repetition limits discovery. Recommendation systems recycle familiar content, trapping users in filter bubbles that reinforce what they already know instead of introducing something new.

Pain Point #2

Emotional disconnect from artists. Discovering music feels transactional. Without storytelling or context, users struggle to form meaningful connections with the artists behind the songs.

Pain Point #3

Context blindness. Platforms ignore mood, environment, and social setting. Recommendations feel generic because the system knows listening history but not the listener's current headspace.

The Insight

Current platforms optimize for prediction, not exploration. Users don't just want the algorithm to guess what they'll like. They want agency in discovering music that matches how they actually feel.

03: The Research

Interviews + Surveys

To understand how people discover and engage with music, we conducted interviews and surveys in September 2024. We received 40 survey responses and conducted 6 in-depth interviews with Georgia Tech students ages 18-24 who regularly use streaming platforms. The insights from these conversations revealed patterns in music discovery habits, emotional responses to recommendations, and social sharing behaviors that shaped our design direction.

Applying Research

Uncovering Themes

To systematically organize insights from our interviews, we applied thematic analysis using coding and affinity mapping. We transcribed interviews in Taguette, tagged recurring patterns, and grouped similar codes in FigJam to identify broader themes. This process helped us move from individual observations to actionable design insights.

Key Themes

From our analysis, three central themes emerged that shaped our design direction:

Theme What We Heard Design Implication
Boredom with Familiar Songs Users reported getting tired of the same recommendations and craved novelty and freshness in their listening experience. Design for serendipity and anti-repetition mechanics
Algorithm Reliance vs. Active Exploration While many users depend on algorithmic suggestions, some actively seek new artists and genres as an intentional, enjoyable activity. Balance passive discovery with exploratory tools that give users agency
Context Matters Users differentiate between casual background listening and focused engagement. Platforms ignore mood, environment, and social setting. Build context-aware features that adapt to how users actually listen

Supporting Codes

Additional patterns validated our initial problem space and revealed unexpected needs:

  • Immersive Triggers: Live experiences (concerts, Spotify DJ) create memorable emotional connections
  • Music as Escape: Users seek music to "fill their mind" and find peace from daily stress
  • Trending Sound Exposure: Viral sounds on social media drive familiarity but contribute to over-standardization
  • Emotional Disconnect: Discovery lacks storytelling, artist context, and personal meaning

04: User Needs

Validating the Problem Space

Through coding and thematic analysis, we confirmed that users actively seek new music but face distinct challenges in how they discover it. Codes related to boredom, mood-driven listening, and artist exploration appeared an average of 3 times per participant, validating our initial problem space.

A Need for Music Novelty

Many participants reported getting bored of familiar songs, wanting music to match their changing moods, and seeking new songs as a way to refresh their playlists and maintain engagement.

The desire to listen to new music manifested in two distinct approaches:

  • Algorithm-Reliant Listeners: Users who primarily depend on algorithmic recommendations or passive discovery through social media and friends.
  • Active Explorers: Users who dedicate time to actively searching for new artists and songs, treating the process as an enjoyable, intentional activity.

A Need for Immersive Listening

Participants value moments of deep, uninterrupted engagement with music. Immersion was often described as a state where the listener is "pulled out of their thoughts" and fully absorbed in the sound.

Concerts and live performances were highlighted as strong triggers of immersion, offering lasting and memorable connections to songs and artists. Participants suggested that such immersive experiences counterbalance the fast-paced, disposable nature of trending sounds, creating opportunities for more meaningful and enduring relationships with music.

Social Media's Influence

We found that users' listening choices are strongly shaped by external influences, both algorithmic (social media posts/Spotify-generated algorithm) and contextual.

These algorithms were described as convenient entry points for discovery, though they can create limitations by reinforcing familiar patterns. Participants also noted the frequency bias, where repeated exposure to a song (through social media's trending reels) increased its likability over time.

Key insight: Over 75% of participants listened to music while walking alone, exercising, or studying. This revealed that music serves different purposes—some use it to shift focus or break from tasks, while others use it to concentrate and enhance work engagement.

03: Our Design Features

Feature: Community Discovery Spaces

Instead of AI-generated suggestions, users can explore the music spheres of people around them, friends, peers, or even strangers nearby. This supports authentic discovery through human taste and shared moments, fulfilling users' need for subtle, meaningful social connection without overwhelming feeds or performative social pressure.

Design Goal: Enable Human-Centered, Non-Algorithmic Social Discovery

Feature: User Spheres

User Spheres create immersive, story-driven spaces crafted by artists and friends. By offering behind-the-scenes content, personal updates, and curated music bubbles that they can share with their fans and friends, this feature directly responds to users' desire for deeper context and stronger emotional ties with the artists they follow.

Design Goal: Deepen Artist-Listener Relationships

Feature: Mood & Environment Spheres

Users reported that algorithmic playlists feel shallow and disconnected from their actual mood or environment. Mood & Environment Spheres use contextual cues (weather, time, location, and listening habits) to generate more personal, emotionally resonant discovery paths. These spheres adapt through interactive 'Vibe checks,' giving users control over how their exploration evolves.

Design Goal: Create Emotionally Intelligent Music Discovery
